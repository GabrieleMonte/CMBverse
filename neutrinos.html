Neutrinos


Since before recombination, Neutrinos have been free-streaming throughout the universe. Their role in the evolution of the Universe is quite significant; thus, by modeling Neutrinos with non-standard characteristics, we can explore the effect they have on the cosmic microwave background.

Although almost massless, Neutrinos still contribute to the matter density of the early universe. Altering this contribution by increasing their intrinsic mass would then increase the amplitude of gravitational potentials. This in turn decreases the relative peak heights, most notably at lower multipoles. Increasing the matter density also shortens the mean free path of photons, which enhances the damping effect at higher multipoles. The increase in matter density also increases the number of structures in the later universe that might deflect the CMB photons via gravitational lensing, increasing the amplitude of the lensing power spectrum, especially at higher multipoles.

If we instead increase the number of radiating neutrinos, we then affect the radiation density of the early universe. Increasing the number of relativistic species increases the photon-baryon interaction rate, causing an increase in photon diffusion length. Additionally, this increase keeps the universe radiation-dominated for longer, which increases the photon-diffusion length and affects the scale at which diffusion operates. These effects enhance the damping on the power spectrum at higher multipoles. Lastly, the decay of potential wells in the radiation-dominated universe is extended, causing an increase in amplitude at low multipoles. This effect is also visible in the lensing power (PP) spectrum, as there are fewer structures for CMB photons to be lensed by.
