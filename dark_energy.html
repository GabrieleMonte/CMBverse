Dark Energy

Dark energy plays a pivotal role in shaping the universe, especially in respects to the CMB, where variation in dark energy density influences the distance to the last-scattering surface. Assuming a flat universe, variations in the initial dark energy density affect this distance by changing the Hubble constant after recombination, as described in the equation below where a is a function of the scale factor and \( H_0 \) is the Hubble constant today:

\({H}^2 (a) = {H}^2_0(\Omega_m a^{-3} + \Omega_\Lambda) \)

When the dark energy density, \( \Omega_\Lambda \), increases at a fixed matter density, \( \Omega_m \)​, the Hubble constant, H, decreases, resulting in a larger distance to the last-scattering. Conversely, if \( \Omega_\Lambda \)​ is decreased, the distance to the last-scattering will also decrease due to the smaller Hubble constant. Furthermore, the increase of \( \Omega_\Lambda \)​ will amplify the power on larger angular scales due to the late Integrated Sachs-Wolfe Effect (ISW). The late ISW effect is set in motion once matter no longer dominates the expansion of the universe, which causes the universe to enter a rapid expansion phase. The onset of dark energy generates a decay of gravitation potential, hence increasing the CMB temperature fluctuations on large scales.
