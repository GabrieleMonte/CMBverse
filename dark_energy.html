Dark Energy

Dark energy plays a pivotal role in shaping the universe, especially in respects to the CMB, where variation in dark energy density influences the distance to the last-scattering surface. Assuming a flat universe, variations in the initial dark energy density affect this distance by changing the Hubble constant after recombination, as described in the equation below where \( a \) is the scale factor and \( H_0 \) is the Hubble constant today:

\({H}^2 (a) = {H}^2_0(\Omega_m a^{-3} + \Omega_\Lambda) \)

When the dark energy density, \( \Omega_\Lambda \), increases at a fixed matter density, \( \Omega_m h^{2}\)​, the Hubble rate, \( H \), increases, resulting in a smaller distance to the last-scattering and an increased angular scale (smaller multiple) of the sound horizon. Conversely, if \( \Omega_\Lambda \)​ is decreased, the distance to the last-scattering will increase due to the smaller Hubble constant and the angular scale will decrease (larger multiple). This manifests as a shift of frequency of the oscillations, as shown in the graphs, and is determined by the angular scale of the sound horizon. Specifically, if \( \Omega_\Lambda \) is increased, the peaks will shift to the left (smaller multiples, larger angular scale), and if \( \Omega_\Lambda \) is decreased, the peaks will shift to the right (larger multiples, smaller angular scale).

Furthermore, the increase of \( \Omega_\Lambda \)​ will amplify the power on larger angular scales due to the late Integrated Sachs-Wolfe Effect (ISW). The onset of dark energy domination generates a decay of gravitation potentials, hence causing photons from the Cosmic Microwave Background to experience less of a gravitational redshift when they exit the wells in comparison to when they entered. As a result of this, the temperature fluctuations increase on larger scales.
