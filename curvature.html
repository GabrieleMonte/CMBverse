Curvature


The peaks in the CMB power spectrum are highly sensitive to the curvature of the early universe, as curvature affects the angular scale of the CMB sound horizon. \(\Omega_{k}\) represents the energy desnity in the universe's curvature. In a flat universe, \(\Omega_{k}\) = 0, the angular scale of the sound horizon should be roughly 1 degree, which aligns with our observations. In an open universe, \(\Omega_{k}\) > 0, with a saddle-shaped geometry, light waves diverge, making the angular size of CMB fluctuations appear smaller and shifting acoustic peaks to smaller angular scales, hence larger multipoles. Conversely, in a closed universe, \(\Omega_{k}\) < 0, resembling a sphere, light rays converge, making fluctuations appearvlarger and shifting peaks to larger angular scales, hence smaller multipoles. The curvature of an open universe would bring a faster rate of expansion, leading to gravitational potentials decaying quicker, enhancing the late ISW effect, and leading to more power at larger scales due to stronger ISW temperature anisotropies. The curvature of a closed universe would bring a slower rate of expansion, leading to gravitational potentials decaying slower, reducing the late ISW effect, and leading to less power at larger scales due to the suppresion of ISW anisotropies.
